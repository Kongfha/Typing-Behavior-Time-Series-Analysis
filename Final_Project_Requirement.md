# 2110430: Final Project

Due: April 24, 2026 @9:00am

The final project is designed to integrate concepts, techniques, and tools covered throughout the course into a real-world time series mining problem. You will formulate a problem, apply appropriate methods, and critically evaluate the results. This is an open-ended project with emphasis on understanding and applying core principles, demonstrating methodological reasoning, and producing insightful analysis.

Group Formation: 3–4 members per group. All members must contribute to the project and presentation.

## Project Scope and Requirements

### 1. Problem Definition

Each group must select a time series mining problem, such as classification, clustering, motif discovery, anomaly detection, forecasting, etc.

### 2. Dataset Selection

You may use public datasets, e.g., UCR Archive, Kaggle, medical/sensor datasets, or collect/simulate your own dataset. The dataset should be moderate in size and sufficiently complex to demonstrate meaningful analysis

### 3. Required Technical Components

Preferably, your project should incorporate at least one core techniques from the course, such as lower bounds, uniform scaling, dimensionality reduction, data augmentation, matrix profile, DTW, similarity search techniques. You can also pick other state-of-the-art approaches, such as Chronos, TimesFM, Deep learning (LSTM, Transformer, etc.), or any others.

### 4. Methodology

You must describe your pipeline: preprocessing, representation, modeling/algorithm, evaluation, etc. Also provide justification for why each method is chosen.

### 5. Experimental Design

Make sure your project includes evaluation metrics appropriate to your task.

### 6. Analysis and Discussion

This is the most important part. You should interpret results (not just report numbers), discuss any strengths/weaknesses/limitations/possible improvements, and discuss impact of techniques (if any, e.g., does lower bound help?)

## Evaluation Criteria

- Problem Definition & Clarity 15%
- Technical Correctness 20%
- Use of Course Concepts 20%
- Originality / Contribution 15%
- Analysis & Insight 20%
- Presentation 10%

## Important Notes:

- Focus on understanding and reasoning, not just implementation
- You are not required to outperform state-of-the-art
- Simpler methods with strong analysis are preferred over complex methods without understanding
- A strong project is not the most complex one, but the one that demonstrates clear understanding, thoughtful design, and meaningful insight
- Proper citation is required for all external sources
- You may use existing libraries and reference implementations, but you must demonstrate understanding and adaptation. Direct reuse without modification or explanation will be penalized.
